Finance May 2025

How AML analysts use data to detect financial crime

A practical look at the data sources, models, and tools used in anti-money laundering investigations.

Anti-money laundering (AML) analysts rely heavily on transaction monitoring systems that flag unusual patterns — large cash deposits, rapid fund movements, or transfers to high-risk jurisdictions. Graph analytics is increasingly used to map relationships between accounts and detect layering schemes. Tools like Actimize, Quantexa, and internal SQL-based rule engines form the backbone of most AML operations. Machine learning models trained on historical SAR (Suspicious Activity Report) data are now being used to reduce false positives — a major pain point in traditional rule-based systems.

Power BI vs Tableau — which should a Business Analyst learn first?

A side-by-side comparison of both tools to help you decide where to invest your learning time.

Power BI wins on Microsoft ecosystem integration — if your org runs on Azure, Teams, and Excel, Power BI is the natural choice and is significantly cheaper at scale. Tableau leads on visual flexibility and is more widely used in data-mature organizations and consulting firms. For a Business Analyst entering the job market, Power BI has more job postings in corporate and mid-market roles, while Tableau dominates in analytics-heavy and enterprise environments. Learn Power BI first if you're targeting banking, consulting, or operations roles. Learn Tableau if you're targeting tech companies or data analyst positions.

SQL Mar 2025

Top SQL queries every Business Analyst uses daily

The essential SQL patterns — from joins to window functions — that show up in real BA work every day.

The queries BAs use most: GROUP BY aggregations for summarizing sales or transaction volumes, LEFT JOIN to combine customer and order tables without losing unmatched rows, CASE WHEN for creating derived categories (e.g. customer tiers), ROW_NUMBER() and RANK() window functions for ranking within groups, and CTEs (WITH clauses) to break complex logic into readable steps. If you master these five patterns, you can handle 80% of the ad-hoc analysis requests a Business Analyst encounters in banking, operations, or finance roles.
